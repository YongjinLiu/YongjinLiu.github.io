Abstract: The core challenge of Few-Shot Fine-Grained Image Classification (FS-FGIC) lies in accurately capturing discriminative representations from limited samples. However, conventional spatial-domain approaches rely heavily on localized saliency, creating a structural blind spot for scenarios where discriminative cues are spatially dispersed and non-contiguously distributed. We observe that while attention-based variants offer theoretical global modeling, their over-parameterized attention maps suffer from high data dependency and optimization volatility under few-shot regimes. Without sufficient data to regularize the attention manifold, these models often collapse onto spurious spatial correlations or background noise, failing to establish stable structural priors for scattered cues. To bridge this gap and simulate these practical challenges, we first construct the Feature-Dispersed Plant Disease Classification (FD-PDC) dataset, providing a rigorous benchmark where features lack definitive focal points. From a methodological perspective, we propose the Frequency-guided Hierarchical Optimization Network (FHNet) to reconcile the limitations of spatial perception by leveraging coordinate-agnostic spectral signatures. FHNet integrates dual-frequency interaction, hierarchical alignment, and support-query mutual learning to transform stochastic spatial fragments into stable frequency-domain representations. This synergy effectively captures non-contiguous textures and regularizes intra-class variance, resolving the optimization hurdles inherent in scattered features. Extensive experiments on both traditional aggregated benchmarks (CUB, Dogs, Cars) and our dispersed FD-PDC dataset demonstrate that FHNet consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods. Our results validate the superiority of frequency-guided perception in bridging the gap between localized spatial bias and global feature dispersion.
